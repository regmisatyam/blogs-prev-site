Quantum-Computing: The Next Future Computer
  • Home
  • Blogs
  • Quantum-Computing

1. What is Quantum Computing?
Quantum computing is a type of computer architecture that uses quantum mechanics to perform calculations. In contrast to classical computers, which use bits (binary digits) to represent data, quantum computers use qubits (quantum binary digits). A qubit is a unit of information that exists in both 0 and 1 states at the same time. Unlike classical computers, which have only two possible values, a qubit can exist in any superposition of these two states simultaneously. This means that a single qubit can hold much more information than a bit.

2. Why do we need Quantum Computers?
The current state-of-the-art classical computers are based on transistors, which are devices that control electrical flow. Transistors are built using silicon, which is a semiconductor material. Silicon is not a good conductor of electricity, so the transistors are isolated from each other using insulators. These insulators limit how fast the transistor can operate. If the speed of the transistor is increased, then the power consumption increases as well. To make things worse, the size of the transistors cannot be decreased indefinitely due to physical limitations.
In order to solve these problems, researchers are working on developing alternative technologies that could potentially replace transistors. One of these technologies is called topological quantum computation. Topological quantum computation is based on the idea that certain materials may exhibit quantum mechanical behavior without having to rely on the laws of thermodynamics. This would allow for faster operations and smaller components.

3. How does Quantum Computing work?
Topological quantum computation relies on the concept of topology. Topology is the branch of mathematics that studies shapes and spaces. In physics, topology describes the fundamental characteristics of space. For example, if you were to take a sphere and cut it in half, the resulting shape is still a sphere. However, if you were to remove a small piece of the sphere, the remaining portion becomes a torus. This is because the sphere was originally a torus. Similarly, if you were to add a tiny hole to a sphere, the result is no longer a sphere. Instead, the sphere now resembles a doughnut. This is because the original sphere had holes inside of it.
This property of topology is what makes topological quantum computation possible. When a particle travels around a closed loop, its path is always continuous. Therefore, the particle's wave function will remain unchanged throughout the entire trip. This means that the particle will return to where it started after completing the loop. Because of this, topological quantum computation is able to achieve faster operations than traditional computers.

4. Where Can We Use Quantum Computing Today?
There are many applications of quantum computing today. One of the most promising fields is cryptography. Cryptography is the practice of encrypting information so that only those who possess the correct keys can decrypt the message. There are several different types of encryption algorithms, including symmetric and asymmetric. Symmetric algorithms use the same key to encrypt and decrypt messages. Asymmetric algorithms use separate keys for encryption and decryption.
Another application of quantum computing is machine learning. Machine learning is the process of teaching computers to learn without being explicitly programmed. This technology is currently used to teach self-driving cars to navigate roads safely. Another potential application of quantum computing is artificial intelligence. Artificial intelligence is the simulation of human intelligence processes, such as reasoning, planning, problem solving, and learning.

5. Is Quantum Computing Ready for Prime Time?
Although quantum computing is still in its infancy, some experts believe that it is ready for prime time. Many companies have already begun investing in research and development. Google recently announced that they are building a $10 million quantum computing lab. IBM has also been researching quantum computing for years. Both companies plan to build their own quantum computers by 2020.
